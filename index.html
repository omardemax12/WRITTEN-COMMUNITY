1 — MANAGEMENT
Q1Define
Define: Situational Analysis.
Answer

Study of the nature, distribution, importance, and contributing factors of health problems in a community, performed at the start of the planning process.

Q2EnumerateA++

  • Step 2: SWOT analysis (internal + external factors)

Q6DiscussA++
Discuss Silicosis: definition, at-risk occupations, pathogenesis, types, and complications.
Answer

Definition: Progressive fibrotic lung disease due to inhalation of crystalline silica.

At-risk: Mining, pottery, glass, foundries, sandblasting, stonecutting.

Pathogenesis: Silica particles damage macrophages → release fibrogenic agents → pulmonary fibrosis.

Types:

TypeExposureDuration
Chronic (commonest)Low exposure>20 years
AcceleratedHigh exposure5 years
Acute (fatal)Very high exposureWeeks–3 years

Complications: TB, respiratory failure, lung cancer, renal failure.

Q8DiscussA++
Discuss Asbestosis: definition, uses, pathogenesis, and complications.
Answer

Definition: Chronic fibrotic lung disease from inhalation of asbestos fibers.

Uses of asbestos: Insulation, construction, protective clothing, roofing, surfacing.

Pathogenesis: Long fibers (>30 µm) not completely engulfed by macrophage → release of lysosomal enzymes → fibroblast stimulation → collagen deposition and pulmonary fibrosis.

Complications:

  • Lung cancer (lower zones; higher risk in smokers)
  • Respiratory failure (pulmonary hypertension, cor pulmonale)
  • Pleural plaques, Mesothelioma, Bronchogenic carcinoma, Laryngeal cancer

Answer
  • Pleural plaques

  • Bronchogenic carcinoma (lung cancer)
  • Laryngeal cancer
Q10DiscussA++
Discuss Coal Workers' Pneumoconiosis (CWP): definition, at-risk occupations, pathophysiology, forms, and progression rate.
Answer

Definition: Lung disease from chronic inhalation of coal dust for over 20 years.

At-risk: Coal miners, handlers, processors.

Pathophysiology: Macrophages engulf coal dust → cytokine release → inflammation → coal macules.

Forms:

  • Simple: Individual coal macules
  • Complicated: Coalescence → progressive massive fibrosis

Progression: 1–2% per year from simple to massive fibrosis.

  • Chest X-ray (ILO classification)
  • Pulmonary function tests (restrictive/obstructive/mixed)
  • CT scan

Q13DiscussA++
Discuss Byssinosis: definition, mechanisms, clinical picture (grading), and diagnosis.
Answer

Definition: Chronic respiratory disease in workers exposed to cotton, flax, hemp, jute, or sisal dust.

Mechanisms:

  • Mechanical irritation of airway epithelium → reflex narrowing
  • Cotton contains histamine → bronchoconstriction; also liberates histamine from platelets
  • Release of bronchoconstrictor mediators (leukotrienes) from PMNs
  • Type III immune complex hypersensitivity
  • Chemotaxis of PMNs
  • Endotoxins from gram-negative bacteria in cotton cell walls
  • Fungal enzymes

Clinical picture (grading):

GradeSymptoms
0No symptoms
1/2Occasional chest tightness on first day of working week
1Chest tightness + breathlessness on first day
2Chest tightness + breathlessness on first day AND other days
3As grade 2 + permanent respiratory disability

Appears after: 20–25 years of exposure.

Diagnosis: Exposure history, typical symptoms, pulmonary function tests.

Q15Enumerate
Enumerate the strategies for prevention and control of occupational lung diseases (Hierarchy of Control).
Answer

Exposure-oriented (Hierarchy of Control):

  • Elimination of hazards
  • Risk reduction (substitution, isolation, engineering controls)
  • Administrative controls (work hours, regulations)
  • Personal protective equipment (masks, respirators)

Work-oriented:

  • Health promotion and smoking prohibition
  • Worker education about hazards
  • Disease surveillance (detect early disease)
  • Control measures (report symptoms, flu vaccination, TB screening in silicosis)

Q3Define
Define: Heatstroke. What is the core temperature threshold?
Answer

A medical emergency characterized by core temperature >41°C, hot dry skin, confusion progressing to coma, absence of sweating, and tachycardia.

Q4Enumerate
Enumerate the skin disorders caused by heat with a brief description of each.
Answer
  • Miliaria (heat rash): Obstruction of sweat gland duct; severity increases with deeper obstruction (vesicles, erythema, desquamation, macules)
  • Intertrigo: Excessive sweating in obese persons; occurs in skin folds (groin, axilla); presents as erythema in macerated skin
  • Heat urticaria: Localized or generalized; appears as wheals surrounded by erythema (hives)
Q5Enumerate
Enumerate the preventive measures for heat-related disorders.
Answer
  • Loose, lightweight clothing
  • Sun protection (hat, sunglasses, sunscreen SPF ≥15)
  • Adequate fluid intake
  • Rest in cool areas; schedule activity in cooler times of day
  • Extra caution for high-risk individuals (medications, chronic illness)
Q6DiscussA++
Discuss Systemic Hypothermia: definition, stages (HT1–HT4), signs, and treatment.
Answer

Definition: Core temperature <35°C; body loses heat faster than it can be replaced.

StageTempSigns

  • Hospital treatment required; cover all extremities
  • Place warm objects (hot packs/water bottles) on head, neck, chest, groin

Q9Discuss
Discuss the standards of potable water: physical, chemical, and bacteriological.
Answer
  • Physical: Odorless, colorless, clear, pleasant taste
  • Chemical: Neutral/slightly alkaline pH, moderate hardness (≤20°); limits: Nitrate 1 ppm, Fluoride 1.5 ppm, Iron 0.3 ppm, Arsenic/Lead = nil
  • Bacteriological: No coliforms or E. coli in 100 ml sample
Q10EnumerateA++
Enumerate the methods of water purification (large-scale and small-scale).
Answer

Large-scale:

  • Filtration (sand on gravel layers — removes bacteria/cysts, NOT viruses; 95% removal)
  • Disinfection (to destroy remaining 5%)
  • Chlorination (dose: 0.6 ppm; residual: 0.2 ppm)
  • Super-chlorination in epidemics

Small-scale:

  • Boiling (kills all pathogens)
  • Domestic filtration (simplest)
  • Disinfection with bleaching powder (20–35% chlorine)
Q11Short Note
Short note on: Chlorination of water.
Answer
  • Dose: 0.6 ppm
  • Residual: 0.2 ppm
  • In epidemics: Super-chlorination is used
  • Necessary because filtration removes only 95% of pathogens (NOT viruses)
Q12Enumerate
Enumerate the agents of water pollution and their health effects.
Answer

  • Oxygen-demanding wastes: Organic matter depleting oxygen → fish death
  • Organic chemicals: Pesticides, oils
  • Sediments: Cloud water, reduce photosynthesis
  • Radioactive isotopes: Birth defects, cancer, genetic damage

Q15DiscussA++

Q16Case
Case 1: A 45-year-old construction worker collapses during outdoor work in August. He has hot dry skin, confusion, and absent sweating. Core temperature is 42°C.
(a) Diagnosis? (b) Immediate management?
Answer

(a) Diagnosis: Heatstroke — Core temp >41°C + hot dry skin + confusion + absent sweating.

(b) Management:

  • Move to shade/cool area immediately
  • Rapid cooling: water, ice packs, fanning
  • Urgent medical care / hospitalization
  • IV fluids as needed
Q17Case
Case 2: A soldier is rescued after hours in cold wet weather. Feet are cold, swollen, numb, and waxy white. Core temperature 36.5°C.
(a) Diagnosis? (b) How does it differ from frostbite? (c) Treatment?
Answer

(a) Immersion Foot (Trench Foot) — prolonged wet/cold exposure above freezing + localized findings + normal core temp.

(b) Vs. Frostbite: Frostbite occurs when skin temp ≤ −3°C → actual ice crystal formation → irreversible tissue damage in extremities. Trench foot is above-freezing wet exposure, no ice crystals.

(c) Treatment:

  • Warm water soak
  • Dry bandages
  • Warm sugary drink
  • Elevation and rest

Q1Define
Define: Health Education.
Answer

Planned learning experiences that provide individuals, groups, and communities with information and skills to make health decisions, leading to changes in knowledge, attitudes, behavior, decision-making, and social change.

  • Directed to all health problem levels (primary, secondary, tertiary)
  • Directed to individuals, families, and communities
  • Helping people make health choices
  • Involves formal and informal teaching
  • Covers a wide range of goals

Q5OutlineA++
Outline the steps of planning a health education program (all components in order).
Answer

  • Assess available resources (funds, human resources, materials, timetable)

5 — GENERAL PREVENTIVE MEASURES OF COMMUNICABLE DISEASES
Q1EnumerateA++
Enumerate the levels of disease prevention.
Answer

  • Primary prevention

  • Tertiary prevention

Q2Define
Define: Primordial Prevention. Give one example.
Answer

Actions to inhibit the emergence of risk factors themselves before they develop — targeting environmental, social, cultural, and behavioral conditions.

Example: Regulating salt content in processed food to prevent hypertension risk factors from emerging in the population.

Q3DiscussA++
Discuss Primary Prevention in communicable diseases: definition, health promotion measures, and specific protection measures.
Answer

Definition: Actions taken before the occurrence of disease to prevent its development in healthy individuals.

Health Promotion (general):

  • Balanced nutrition, physical exercise, recreation
  • Sanitary environment: clean air, safe water, food hygiene, vector control, rodent control, vaccination of stray animals
  • Health education and awareness

Specific Protection:

  • Vaccination: Provides immunity + builds herd immunity
  • Seroprophylaxis: Ready-made immunoglobulins/antitoxins for rapid short-term protection
  • Chemoprophylaxis: Antimicrobials to prevent disease (e.g., penicillin for rheumatic fever, INH for TB, tetracyclines for cholera, ciprofloxacin for meningitis)

International Measures:

  • Quarantine for: cholera, TB, diphtheria, plague, smallpox, yellow fever, VHF, SARS, pandemic flu, measles, COVID-19
  • Animal quarantine from endemic areas
  • Disinfection certificates for imported goods
  • Deratting certificates for ships; disinfection of planes from endemic zones
Q4Define
Define: Seroprophylaxis. How does it differ from Chemoprophylaxis?
Answer

Seroprophylaxis: Administration of ready-made immunoglobulins or antitoxins (animal or human origin) for rapid, short-term passive protection after exposure.

Chemoprophylaxis: Use of antimicrobial drugs to prevent disease development in an exposed or high-risk individual.

SeroprophylaxisChemoprophylaxis
Agent usedImmunoglobulins / antitoxinsAntimicrobial drugs
Immunity typePassiveNone (prevents multiplication)
ExampleRabies IG after biteINH for TB contacts

  • Yellow fever

Q6DiscussA++
Discuss Secondary Prevention measures in communicable diseases.
Answer
  • Control of cases: Case finding, notification, isolation, concurrent and terminal disinfection, treatment, release
  • Control of carriers: Pre-employment and periodic exams for food handlers, medical staff, childcare workers
  • Control of contacts: Enlistment, examination, surveillance, segregation/isolation, immunization or chemoprophylaxis
  • Control of animal reservoirs: Eradication, sanitary raising, veterinary care
  • Community measures: Surveillance, eradication, elimination of disease
Q7Enumerate
Enumerate the steps of control of contacts in secondary prevention.
Answer
  • Enlistment (identify all contacts)
  • Examination
  • Surveillance
  • Segregation or isolation
  • Immunization or chemoprophylaxis
Q8Discuss
Discuss Tertiary Prevention measures in communicable diseases.
Answer
  • Reduce or eliminate long-term impairments and disabilities
  • Minimize suffering and optimize function
  • Prevent further deterioration
  • Help complicated cases cope with handicaps and live independently
Q9Define
Define: Quaternary Prevention.
Answer

Actions taken to protect patients from over-medicalization and ensure only ethically acceptable interventions are applied. Avoids unnecessary investigations, medications, and procedures.

Q1Case — Environment
Water Case: After flooding, multiple cases of acute diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration over 3 days. All ages and sexes. Common unchlorinated water supply suspected.
(a) Criteria confirming waterborne epidemic? (b) Possible biological agents? (c) Immediate control? (d) Large-scale water purification?
Answer

(a) Waterborne epidemic criteria (all present here):

  • Sudden onset
  • Many cases in few days
  • Common water source
  • All ages and sexes affected
  • Multiple family cases
  • Any season (flooding → any time)

(b) Possible biological agents:

  • Bacteria: Vibrio cholerae, Shigella, Salmonella
  • Viruses: Hepatitis A, Poliovirus, Rotavirus
  • Parasites: Giardia, Cryptosporidium

(c) Immediate control:

  • Identify and treat the contaminated water source
  • Super-chlorinate the water supply
  • Neutralize excess chlorine
  • Monitor for secondary cases
  • ORS/IV rehydration for affected cases

(d) Large-scale purification:

  • Filtration (sand on gravel — removes 95% of bacteria/cysts)
  • Chlorination (0.6 ppm dose; 0.2 ppm residual)
  • Super-chlorination in epidemic settings
Q2Case — Environment
Mercury Case: Coastal fishing community — neurological symptoms (tremors, memory loss, sensory disturbances) in adults + developmental delays in children. Main diet: large predatory fish from an industrially contaminated bay.
(a) Type of poisoning + classical historical example? (b) Mechanism of mercury accumulation in fish? (c) Health effects? (d) Preventive measures?
Answer

(a) Methyl mercury poisoning (environmental) — Classical example: Minamata disease (Japan, 1950s — industrial mercury discharged into Minamata Bay → fish → community).

(b) Mechanism — Bioaccumulation/Biomagnification: Mercury from industrial sources is converted to methyl mercury by aquatic bacteria → absorbed by small fish → concentrated in larger predatory fish → highest levels at top of food chain (tuna, swordfish, shark).

(c) Health effects:

  • Neurological damage: tremors, memory loss, sensory disturbances, ataxia
  • Birth defects and developmental delays in children (prenatal exposure)
  • Cancer and genetic damage

(d) Preventive measures:

  • Regulate industrial discharge into water bodies
  • Avoid consumption of large predatory fish (especially for pregnant women and children)
  • Regular monitoring of fish mercury levels
  • Health education for fishing communities
  • Avoid broken thermometers, dental amalgams where alternatives exist
